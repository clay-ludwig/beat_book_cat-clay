**STORY 2: Ballots not cast online**

*   Rich Adrian clarified that Maryland ballots printed from the website cannot be cast online.
*   Voters fill out the printed ballot and mail it in.
*   There is no voting online, eliminating hacking concerns.
*   The printed ballot is treated with the same scrutiny as any other mail-in ballot.

**STORY 7: Immigrants contributing taxes**

*   Over 8 million unauthorized US workers contributed $73 billion in federal taxes in 2022 using ITIN numbers.
*   These workers also paid $33.9 billion in payroll deductions to Medicare, Social Security, and unemployment insurance.
*   The Trump Administration’s deportation campaign discourages voluntary tax payment.
*   Over 10 million unauthorized immigrants remain in the US, working and contributing to communities without a path to legal residency or citizenship.

**STORY 8: Republicans continue to push Talbot Board of Elections on election judge breakdown**

*   County Republicans continued to push the Talbot County Board of Elections on election judge breakdown.
*   The current breakdown is 62 Democratic judges, 50 Republican judges, and 11 unaffiliated judges.
*   Local officials cited state law requiring equal numbers of judges from each party.
*   State agencies emphasized election security and compliance with law.
*   Attorney General Anthony Brown said it's not possible to achieve exact party balance due to factors outside the local board's control.
*   The Board of Elections accepts interest forms for election judges continuously and provides training.

**STORY 9: St. Michaels Police Chief says salaries need to be raised for officer recruitment, retention**

*   St. Michaels Police Chief Jeffrey Oswald said the police department will lose an 18-year veteran officer due to better pay.
*   The St. Michaels Police Department operates with four officers.
*   Three officers left, including one for a larger department's higher salary and opportunities.
*   Town Manager Don Richardson suggested hiring a recruiting firm and raising wages, with potential costs of over $200,000.
*   Commissioners agreed to draft legislation allowing for salary amendments to make the police department competitive in hiring and retention.

**STORY 10: Talbot’s advanced life support pioneers reflect on program’s 40-year mark**

*   Five Easton firefighters began an advanced life support program on August 13, 1985.
*   The “first five” pioneers included Wayne Dyott, Kevin Knussman, Marc Stockley, Ray Taylor, and the late Gary Myers.
*   The program has benefited Talbot County residents for 40 years.
